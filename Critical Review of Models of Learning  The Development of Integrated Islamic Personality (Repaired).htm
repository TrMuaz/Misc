Critical Review of Models of Learning: The Development of Integrated Islamic Personality

An Islamic Educational Psychology Perspective

Executive Summary

This report critically examines major Western models of learning — Behaviorism, Cognitivism, Constructivism, Humanism, and Social Learning Theory — through the lens of Islamic educational psychology, assessing both their compatibility with and limitations within an Islamic worldview. It then synthesises frameworks for an Integrated Islamic Personality (IIP), grounded in the foundational concepts of tawhid (oneness of God), fitrah (innate human nature), nafs–qalb–ruh–aql (the four psycho-spiritual dimensions), and the educational paradigms of tarbiyah, ta'lim, and ta'dib. Drawing on classical scholars — Al-Ghazali, Ibn Sina, Ibn Khaldun, and Syed Muhammad Naquib Al-Attas — as well as contemporary Islamic psychology and educational thought, this report proposes that the ultimate goal of Islamic education is the formation of insan kamil (the complete human being), a holistic personality that unifies intellectual excellence, moral virtue, spiritual depth, and social responsibility.

1. Introduction

Islamic educational psychology occupies a distinctive position at the intersection of divine revelation, classical Islamic scholarship, and the modern psychological sciences. Unlike purely secular frameworks, it situates learning within a cosmological vision in which the human being (insan) is created by Allah as His vicegerent (khalifah) on earth, endowed with an innate pure nature (fitrah), and accountable for the development of every faculty entrusted to them.[1][2]

Contemporary Muslim scholars and educators have increasingly recognised that the uncritical adoption of Western learning models — developed largely within secular, materialist, or empiricist traditions — creates epistemological tensions for Muslim learners and educators alike. These tensions are not merely theoretical: they shape curriculum design, pedagogical practice, student motivation, and the moral formation of graduates.[3][4][^5]

This report addresses these tensions systematically. It begins by mapping each major Western learning theory, then subjects each to critical scrutiny from an Islamic perspective, and finally builds toward a synthesised model of integrated Islamic personality development that is both theoretically robust and practically applicable.

2. Conceptual Foundations of Islamic Educational Psychology

2.1 The Islamic View of the Human Being

Islamic educational psychology begins with a comprehensive anthropology derived from the Qur'an and Sunnah. The human being is understood as a multidimensional entity comprising the body (jism), the intellect (aql), the soul (nafs), the heart (qalb), and the spirit (ruh). These are not separate, unrelated faculties but are deeply interconnected constituents of a unified self, each requiring development through education.[6][7]

·        Nafs (Soul/Psyche): The seat of self, ego, and moral agency. Al-Ghazali describes three levels: nafs al-ammarah (the commanding self driven by desire), nafs al-lawwamah (the self-reproaching, conscience-driven self), and nafs al-mutma'innah (the tranquil self at peace with God). Education in Islam fundamentally aims to elevate the nafs from its lower states to a state of spiritual tranquillity.[^8]

·        Qalb (Heart): The spiritual heart, the locus of moral and spiritual consciousness. Al-Ghazali identifies the qalb as the organ through which divine knowledge (ilm ladunni) may be received, provided it is purified of spiritual diseases such as pride, envy, and heedlessness.[^9]

·        Aql (Intellect): The rational faculty that distinguishes humans from animals and is the instrument for understanding divine signs (ayat) in the universe and the scripture. Islamic tradition greatly honours the intellect but subordinates it to revelation, not in opposition to reason, but as its ultimate guide.[^2]

·        Ruh (Spirit): The divine breath (nafkha) infused into the human being at creation (Qur'an 15:29), whose ultimate nature is known only to God. In Islamic psychology, the ruh is the animating principle that links the human to the transcendent dimension.[^10]

2.2 The Concept of Fitrah

The concept of fitrah is foundational to Islamic educational psychology. Derived from the Arabic root fatara (to create or originate), fitrah refers to the innate God-given disposition with which every human being is born — a natural orientation toward recognising and submitting to the oneness of Allah and adhering to moral and ethical values. Ibn Taymiyyah describes it as "the natural state of the human being, in accordance with which he is created," while Al-Ghazali defines it as "the original purity of the human being, which inclines him towards recognising his Creator".[^11]

From an educational standpoint, fitrah implies that learning is not the mere inscription of knowledge on a blank slate (contra Locke's tabula rasa) but the nurturing of innate potential. Islamic education, therefore, functions as the cultivation, not creation, of goodness — aligning the learner's God-given capacities with divine guidance. This has profound implications for curriculum design, pedagogy, and the teacher-student relationship, situating the educator as a guide who awakens what is already latently present, rather than an external programmer.[12][1]

2.3 The Principle of Tawhid as Educational Epistemology

Tawhid — the affirmation of the oneness of Allah — is not merely a theological doctrine but the epistemological and spiritual foundation of the entire Islamic knowledge system. All disciplines of knowledge — whether natural sciences, social sciences, or the humanities — are understood as investigations into the signs (ayat) of Allah and thus intrinsically unified under a divine canopy. A tawhid-based curriculum does not fragment knowledge into "religious" and "secular" domains; rather, it integrates all learning within a framework of divine purposefulness.[13][14][15][16][^17]

Ismail Raji Al-Faruqi formalised this vision through his "Islamization of Knowledge" project, arguing that the duality between Islamic and Western knowledge in Muslim education must be overcome by reconstructing disciplines from a tawhid foundation. This pedagogical philosophy has direct implications for the development of integrated Islamic personality: a learner who understands knowledge as unified under God's sovereignty is equipped to integrate spiritual, intellectual, and moral dimensions of selfhood.[18][19][^20]

3. Critical Review of Western Models of Learning from an Islamic Perspective

3.1 Behaviorism

Overview: Behaviorism — pioneered by Pavlov (classical conditioning), Watson, Thorndike, and Skinner (operant conditioning) — posits that learning is a change in observable behaviour produced by stimulus-response associations and reinforcement contingencies. The learner is viewed as a passive recipient shaped by environmental inputs, and internal mental states (let alone spiritual ones) are deemed scientifically irrelevant.[25][3]

Islamic Critique:

The philosophical underpinnings of behaviorism are fundamentally at odds with the Islamic worldview. J. B. Watson explicitly sought to treat humans as animals "without something else in addition" — this "something else" being precisely what Islam holds most sacred: din (religion), akhirah (the hereafter), akhlaq (moral character), and the ruh (spirit). Skinner's radical behaviorism, by reducing all behaviour to conditioned responses to tangible rewards and punishments, renders Islamic concepts of akhlaq, ibadah, and tawhid into mere illusions of reinforcement schedules — a position that directly contradicts Islamic aqidah.[^26]

Furthermore, behaviorism cannot account for profound transformative learning events that Muslims experience — such as the radical behavioural change following hajj (pilgrimage), conversion to Islam, or deep dhikr (remembrance of God). These phenomena require acknowledgement of the soul and the reality of iman (faith) as moderating variables.[^26]

Partial Compatibility: Despite its philosophical deficiencies, certain principles of classical and operant conditioning are not foreign to Islam. The Qur'an itself (5:4) references the conditioning of animals; the Prophet Muhammad (SAW) employed principles of gradual reinforcement in the four-stage prohibition of alcohol, which bears striking resemblance to systematic desensitisation. Al-Ghazali and Ibn Sina both recognised stimulus-response association but added cognitive and spiritual moderators that contemporary behaviorism lacked: imagination, memory, and the soul's level of iman. Ibn Sina specifically argued that association must be retained in memory to function as a conditioned stimulus — a cognitive refinement of Pavlovian conditioning developed centuries earlier.[27][26]

Islamic Modification: The soul (nafs) and its level of iman must be inserted as the central moderating variable in any conditioning-based explanation of Muslim learning. Islamic rewards and punishments differ from Skinnerian ones in being intangible, delayed (extending to the hereafter), and spiritually mediated — making faith itself the most powerful reinforcer for a sincere Muslim.[^26]

3.2 Cognitivism

Overview: Cognitive learning theories — including Piaget's developmental stages, information processing, schema theory, and insight learning — shift attention from observable behaviour to internal mental processes: perception, memory, thinking, problem-solving, and understanding. The learner is an active processor of information who constructs mental representations of the world.[28][25]

Islamic Critique:

Cognitivism's emphasis on the aql (intellect) resonates significantly with Islamic epistemology, which strongly honours rational inquiry and the use of the mind to reflect on divine signs (tafakkur). Al-Ghazali's treatment of insight learning (ilm ladunni — intuitive God-given knowledge) anticipates and transcends contemporary cognitive categories, describing forms of learning that cannot be explained without reference to the soul and its relationship to divine inspiration (ilham).[28][26]

However, cognitivism remains limited because it situates all knowledge acquisition within the immanent cognitive apparatus of the individual, excluding both the transcendent source of ilahi (divine) learning and the spiritual dimensions of the qalb and ruh. Insight learning in its Islamic form extends beyond Gestaltian restructuring of problem representations to include Qur'anic examples of direct inspiration to non-prophets — the mother of Prophet Musa (AS), the disciples of Prophet Isa (AS) — all of which require an epistemology that accepts revealed and inspired knowledge as legitimate categories.[3][26]

Partial Compatibility: The emphasis on active mental processing aligns with the Qur'anic imperative for tafakkur (reflection), tadabbur (deep contemplation of scripture), and taqaqqul (rational comprehension). Al-Ghazali's cognitive additions to conditioning theory — the roles of memory, imagination, and attention — anticipate modern cognitive psychology by centuries.[2][28]

Islamic Modification: Cognitivism must be expanded to include two additional epistemological sources beyond rational and empirical knowing: wahy (revelation — exclusive to prophets) and ilham (inspiration — accessible to believers of high iman). A complete Islamic cognitive model acknowledges that the highest form of insight (kashf — spiritual unveiling) is available to those whose hearts are purified, representing a vertical epistemic channel that purely horizontal cognitive models cannot accommodate.[9][26]

3.3 Constructivism

Overview: Constructivism — rooted in Piaget and extended by Vygotsky (social constructivism) — holds that learners actively construct knowledge through experience, reflection, and social interaction, rather than passively receiving transmitted information. The teacher serves as a facilitator who scaffolds the learner's construction of meaning.[29][30]

Islamic Critique:

Constructivism is perhaps the Western theory most compatible with Islamic pedagogy in its methodological dimensions. The Qur'anic call for tafakkur (reflection on the universe), tadabbur (reflection on scripture), and ijtihad (independent reasoning within Islamic scholarship) resonates with constructivism's insistence on active, meaning-making learning. The tradition of halaqah (learning circles) in classical Islamic education represents a naturally constructivist, dialogical pedagogy.[29][28]

Vygotsky's social constructivism aligns with the Islamic emphasis on learning within the community (ummah), consultation (shura), and the concept of collective intellectual development. The Prophetic practice of the Suffah — a learning community in Madinah where companions learned collectively and mutually — exemplifies cooperative, socially scaffolded learning long before Vygotsky articulated its theoretical basis.[31][30][^32]

Limitations: Constructivism's relativism concerning truth — the notion that all knowledge is "constructed" and therefore contextual — is problematic from an Islamic perspective. Islamic epistemology distinguishes between haqq (absolute truth from divine revelation) and zann (opinion or provisional human knowledge). Not all knowledge is equally constructed: divine revelation is objective, authoritative, and non-negotiable. A purely constructivist curriculum in Islamic education risks undermining the authority of wahy and blurring the distinction between absolute and relative knowledge.[^16]

Islamic Modification: Constructivism can be Islamically adapted by situating active knowledge construction within a framework of fixed divine principles. The learner constructs understanding of the sciences, social realities, and ethical problems through experience and reflection — but always guided by the Qur'an and Sunnah as the ultimate epistemological anchors.[4][29]

3.4 Humanism

Overview: Humanistic psychology — exemplified by Abraham Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs and Carl Rogers' person-centred theory — emphasises the inherent goodness and self-actualising potential of the human being. The highest goal of education, in Maslow's framework, is self-actualisation — the realisation of one's unique human potential.[^33]

Islamic Critique:

The humanistic elevation of the human being as the measure of all things and the definition of self-actualisation in purely immanent terms represents a fundamental tension with the Islamic worldview, which situates human dignity within the frame of divine trusteeship (amanah) and servitude (ubudiyyah). Maslow's model, as critiqued in the Intellectual Discourse journal (IIUM, 2016), systematically excludes the spiritual dimension of human existence — the very dimension that Islamic psychology regards as primary.[34][35][^33]

Specifically, Maslow's original five-tier model treats religion as a secondary psychosocial phenomenon subsumed under "belongingness" or "esteem" needs, rather than as the foundational orientation of the human being. In Islamic anthropology, ibadah (worship) and tawakkul (reliance on God) are not peripheral needs but the fundamental purpose of human existence (Qur'an 51:56). A Muslim's "self-actualisation" cannot be understood without reference to the akhirah (hereafter) and the aspiration to taqwa (God-consciousness) — goals that transcend Maslow's immanent framework entirely.[36][34]

Partial Compatibility: Carl Rogers' concept of the empathic, congruent, unconditionally accepting facilitator resonates with classical Islamic descriptions of the ideal educator: the murabbi (spiritual mentor) who nurtures the learner with compassion, recognises individual differences, and creates a trusting relational environment. The Integrative Islamic Personality Model (IIPM) draws precisely on Rogers' self-theory to develop its construct of "Self-regard," integrating it with Said Nursi's Islamic framework.[37][38][^39]

Islamic Modification: The Islamic analogue of self-actualisation is insan kamil — the complete human being who actualises both divine and human potentials through iman (faith), ilm (knowledge), and amal salih (righteous action). Unlike Maslow's model, the Islamic hierarchy places the relationship with Allah (aqidah and ibadah) at the apex, from which all other human needs and aspirations derive their meaning and proper ordering.[40][36]

3.5 Social Learning Theory

Overview: Albert Bandura's Social Learning Theory (1977) holds that humans learn by observing, imitating, and modelling others' behaviours, mediated by cognitive processes of attention, retention, reproduction, and motivation. The model (person observed) plays a pivotal role in transmitting behaviour, values, and competencies.[31][3]

Islamic Critique:

Social learning theory enjoys the highest degree of compatibility with Islamic educational thought among Western models, primarily because Islam places extraordinary emphasis on the role of the Prophet Muhammad (SAW) as the uswatun hasanah — the most perfect exemplar for all humanity (Qur'an 33:21). The entire Prophetic pedagogical method was modelled on qudwah (exemplary conduct): companions learned salat (prayer), hajj rituals, interpersonal ethics, and governance directly by observing the Prophet.[31][26]

However, Bandura's model has important empirical and conceptual limitations when applied to Islamic learning. Research shows that observational learning accounts for only 22–24% of variance in delinquency and 6% in drug use — the strongest predictor being moral disengagement, which points toward the soul factor. Furthermore, the theory cannot explain how contemporary Muslims model the Prophet's behaviour from a biography 14 centuries old — a form of "virtual observational learning" that requires iman in the soul as a pre-condition of receptivity.[^26]

The Qur'anic examples of the wives of Prophets Nuh (AS) and Lut (AS) — who failed to model their prophet husbands — and the wife of Pharaoh — who modelled the right path despite a tyrannical husband — demonstrate that soul and iman are more powerful determinants of behaviour than the proximate model, a finding social learning theory cannot accommodate.[^26]

Islamic Modification: The teacher (murabbi) in Islamic education functions not merely as a social model but as a spiritual guide who shapes the student's soul through lived example, tarbiyah, and tazkiyat al-nafs. The Islamic extension of social learning theory thus incorporates a vertical (divine-human) dimension of modelling alongside Bandura's horizontal (human-human) one, recognising that the Prophet (SAW) as model can only be meaningfully accessed through iman-informed engagement with hadith and seerah.[41][42][31][26]

4.2 Tazkiyat Al-Nafs: The Core Mechanism of Islamic Learning

The central learning mechanism in Al-Ghazali's framework is tazkiyat al-nafs — the purification of the soul — which proceeds through three stages:[^43]

1.      Takhalli (Emptying): The systematic removal of moral vices (ratha'il al-akhlaq) — pride, envy, hypocrisy, excessive love of worldly things — from the soul through muhasabah (self-accounting) and mujahada (spiritual struggle).

2.     Tahalli (Adorning): The cultivation of virtues (fada'il al-akhlaq) — sincerity (ikhlas), patience (sabr), gratitude (shukr), humility (tawadu) — through riyadat al-nafs (spiritual exercises and discipline).

3.      Tajalli (Illumination): The resulting state of spiritual illumination and kashf (unveiling) in which the purified heart becomes receptive to higher knowledge and divine guidance.[44][43]

This three-stage model has direct pedagogical implications. A learner whose soul is diseased with pride, envy, or excessive materialism will be fundamentally incapacitated in their pursuit of genuine knowledge, regardless of their intellectual capacity. Al-Ghazali's framework thus positions moral and spiritual development not as a supplement to intellectual education but as its prerequisite condition.[8][9]

4.3 The Role of Niyyah (Intention) in Learning

A distinctive and irreplaceable element of the Islamic model of learning is niyyah — the sincere intention behind action. Unlike Western motivational theories that focus on external reinforcement (operant conditioning) or intrinsic goal-orientation (self-determination theory), Islamic educational psychology positions niyyah as the spiritual foundation that determines not only the quality but the divine acceptability of all learning activity.[^45]

The Prophetic hadith, "Actions are judged by intentions" (Innama al-a'malu bil-niyyat), is the cornerstone of Islamic motivational theory. When a learner's intention in seeking knowledge is to please Allah (li-wajhillah), the very act of learning becomes ibadah (worship), generating spiritual reward alongside intellectual growth. This transforms the classroom into a sacred space and elevates education from a social utility to a divine obligation.[46][45]

5. Ibn Sina and Ibn Khaldun: Complementary Educational Theories

5.1 Ibn Sina (Avicenna, 980–1037 CE)

Ibn Sina's educational philosophy, elaborated in al-Siyasa and al-Qanun, presents a staged developmental model anticipating elements of modern cognitive development theory. He argued that education must be directed toward three dimensions: physical development, intellectual cultivation, and moral character (adab). Crucially, Ibn Sina understood morality as an acquired quality, not inborn — attainable through habituation, imitation, fear, and wisdom — placing him as a precursor of both social learning and cognitive-developmental theory.[47][48]

Ibn Sina's cognitive contribution to learning theory was his insistence that association between stimuli requires memory and imagination as mediating cognitive processes — an anticipation of cognitive mediation that Watson and Pavlov later omitted from their mechanistic accounts. His view that education should match a student's individual talents and tendencies resonates with contemporary differentiated instruction and learning style theories.[47][27][^26]

5.2 Ibn Khaldun (1332–1406 CE)

Ibn Khaldun's Muqaddimah contains a remarkably sophisticated theory of pedagogy grounded in social, cultural, and psychological insights. His concept of the master-pupil relationship emphasises that severe punishment in the course of instruction harms the student, especially children, causing oppression of spirit and loss of energy — an anticipation of Rogers' person-centred pedagogy and Bandura's insights on negative reinforcement.[^27]

Ibn Khaldun's theory of 'asabiyyah (social solidarity) applied to education anticipates Vygotsky's sociocultural theory: learning is embedded in social and communal contexts, shaped by collective belonging and shared identity. His recognition that the acquisition of knowledge is a gradual, cumulative process requiring environmental and social scaffolding remains a sophisticated contribution to educational psychology.[2][47]

6.2 The Islamic Personality Model (IPM) — Extended Framework

Building on the IIPM, contemporary scholars propose a broader Islamic Personality Model (IPM) that integrates six dimensions of holistic development:[^40]

1.      Spiritual Dimension (Ruhiyyah): Iman, taqwa, ibadah, and proximity to Allah

2.     Moral Dimension (Akhlaqiyyah): Husn al-akhlaq (excellence of character), honesty, justice, compassion

3.      Intellectual Dimension (Aqliyyah): Critical thinking, tafakkur, mastery of fardhu 'ain and fardhu kifayah knowledge

4.     Social Dimension (Ijtima'iyyah): Ukhuwwah (brotherhood), muamalat, contribution to ummah

5.      Personal/Psychological Dimension (Nafsiyyah): Emotional regulation, sabr, tawakkul, mental well-being

6.     Behavioural Dimension (Sulukiyyah): Outward behaviour conforming to shari'ah, consistent with inner conviction

Researchers at Frontiers in Psychology (2025) frame this holistic framework around Husn-i-Akhlaaq — Islamic character refinement — positioning it as the mechanism of achieving the highest human potential, integrating ruh, qalb, and nafs in a structured process of moral and spiritual growth drawing from Rumi's Insan-i Kamil and Iqbal's Mard-i-Momin.[^36]

7. Insan Kamil: The Ultimate Goal of Islamic Education

The concept of insan kamil (the complete or perfect human being) represents the teleological goal toward which all Islamic learning and personality development aspires. This concept integrates all dimensions of the Islamic personality into a unified vision: a human being who embodies iman (faith), ilm (knowledge), amal salih (righteous action), akhlaq karimah (noble character), and khilafah (vicegerency of God on earth) simultaneously and authentically.[50][51][^52]

Contemporary scholar Quraish Shihab interprets insan kamil as individuals who represent divine values in real life, making the Prophet Muhammad (SAW) an ideal pedagogical model. This is not an abstract mystical concept but a practical educational orientation: the process of education, curriculum design, teaching methodology, and student assessment should all be shaped by the question of whether they are producing graduates who embody the insan kamil ideal.[51][50]

The insan kamil concept offers a profound critique of contemporary education's overemphasis on cognitive attainment at the expense of moral and spiritual formation. As Shihab argues, "knowledge without moral formation produces intelligent but unwise individuals" — a description that fits the crisis of moral degradation, identity confusion, and psychological distress documented among Muslim youth educated in purely secular or narrowly intellectualist frameworks.[1][4][^50]

8. The Role of the Teacher in Integrated Islamic Personality Development

8.1 The Murabbi: Educator as Spiritual Mentor

The teacher in Islamic education occupies a multidimensional role that Western educational models do not fully capture. Beyond the mu'allim (knowledge transmitter) and mudarris (classroom instructor), the Islamic tradition foregrounds the murabbi — the spiritual mentor who nurtures the student's soul, models divine values through lived conduct, and guides the learner's inner formation.[42][41]

Al-Ghazali, in Ihya Ulum al-Din, describes teachers as "the heirs of the prophets," bearing moral and spiritual responsibility for guiding students toward closeness to Allah. Ibn Miskawaih similarly argues that the morals and spirituality of an educator influence the souls of students "just as fire influences iron" — a process of spiritual induction that transcends verbal instruction.[^42]

Contemporary research on murabbi confirms that students' spiritual intelligence is formed through three dimensions of teacher modelling: (1) ibadah modelling — teachers performing congregational dhuha prayer, reciting the Qur'an in students' presence; (2) moral exemplarity — displaying humility, compassion, and emotional regulation; and (3) spiritual discipline — consistency in worship practices and spiritual motivation.[41][42]

8.2 Pedagogical Methods of the Prophet Muhammad (SAW)

The Prophet Muhammad (SAW) remains the supreme model for Islamic pedagogy, having employed a comprehensive range of teaching strategies that contemporary educational psychology is only beginning to systematically describe:[^31]

·        Exemplary modelling (qudwah): Companions learned ritual acts by direct observation of the Prophet

·        Dialogue and questioning (hiwar): Socratic-style exchanges that activate critical thinking

·        Storytelling (qasas): Narrative engagement that stimulates emotional and cognitive learning

·        Gradual instruction (tadarruj): Progressive revelation of knowledge matched to the learner's capacity

·        Recognition of individual differences: Personalised guidance based on each companion's personality

·        Positive reinforcement (al-targhib): Motivation through promise of divine reward before warning of punishment[^31]

These strategies anticipate key principles of cognitive psychology (scaffolding), differentiated instruction, narrative learning, and humanistic positive reinforcement, while embedding them within the irreducible context of divine purpose.[4][31]

9. Toward a Synthesised Islamic Learning Model

9.1 The Alias–Abdul Majid Islamic Learning Model

Alizi Alias and Hariyati Shahrima Abdul Majid (IIUM, 2015) propose the most systematic Islamic model of learning to date, integrating all major Western theories as partial contributors to an Islamic whole, moderated by the soul (nafs) and iman (faith). The model posits a hierarchical series of questions that a learning stimulus must answer positively to produce an Islamically grounded behavioural change:[3][26]

1.      Do physiological factors provide potential for this learning?

2.     Do instinctual needs or personality variables provide motivation?

3.      Is the behaviour consistent with Islamic aqidah, shari'ah, and akhlaq?

4.     Do inspirations (ilham) or divine guidance indicate the behaviour should be performed?

5.      Does the stimulus-response relationship exist (moderated by cognition)?

6.     Do significant/influential others (models) perform the behaviour?

The soul (nafs) functions as the central moderator across all these levels — the variable that determines whether and how each partial theoretical explanation actually operates in the life of a Muslim learner.[^26]

9.2 The Aqliyyah-Nafsiyyah Taxonomy

Contemporary Malaysian scholars propose the Aqliyyah-Nafsiyyah taxonomy as an Islamic alternative to Bloom's taxonomy for Islamic Religious Education, drawing on the works of Ibn Sina, Al-Ghazali, and Al-Attas. This taxonomy integrates cognitive (aqliyyah) and spiritual (nafsiyyah) dimensions of learning into a unified framework that addresses the unique demands of Islamic education — demands that Bloom's purely cognitive taxonomy, however useful, cannot fully capture.[^53]

10. Challenges and Contemporary Implications

10.1 The Dualism Problem

The most persistent challenge in Islamic educational psychology is the enduring dualism between "religious" and "secular" knowledge in Muslim educational institutions. This dualism — a colonial legacy in many Muslim-majority societies — creates split personalities: students who are intellectually equipped for modern professional life but spiritually and morally underdeveloped. The resolution of this dualism through tawhid-based integration is not merely an academic project but an urgent educational priority.[17][55][^18]

10.2 Moral Degradation and Identity Crisis

Contemporary Islamic educational psychology research consistently identifies moral degradation, stress, and identity crises as pressing challenges among Muslim students educated in systems that neglect the spiritual dimension. Islamic Educational Psychology, with its emphasis on tazkiyat al-nafs, the role of murabbi, and the formation of insan kamil, provides a theoretically grounded and practically applicable response to these challenges.[1][4][^2]

10.3 The Islamization of Psychology

The project of Islamizing educational psychology — initiated by Al-Faruqi's Islamization of Knowledge and carried forward by institutions like IIUM — requires moving beyond mere criticism of Western theories toward the construction of positive, empirically testable Islamic frameworks. Current efforts include the development of the IIPM, the IPM, the Aqliyyah-Nafsiyyah taxonomy, and various instruments for assessing Islamic personality dimensions.[5][37][17][53][^40]

11. Conclusion

The critical review of Western models of learning through Islamic educational psychology reveals a consistent pattern: each theory captures a partial truth about human learning, but all fail at the same point — the exclusion of the soul, iman, and the transcendent dimension of the human being from their explanatory frameworks. Behaviorism misses the soul altogether; cognitivism misses divine inspiration; constructivism relativises truth; humanism sublimates worship into self-actualisation; and social learning theory underestimates the power of iman as a moderating variable in imitation and modelling.

Islamic educational psychology does not simply reject these theories but critically integrates their valid insights into a larger framework — one centred on the fitrah of the human being, the unity of knowledge under tawhid, the purification of the soul through tazkiyat al-nafs, and the cultivation of adab toward the Creator, creation, and oneself. The goal of this integrated framework is the formation of insan kamil — the complete human being — who unifies intellectual excellence, moral nobility, spiritual depth, and social responsibility in a life oriented toward the pleasure of Allah and the flourishing of humanity.

This is not a theoretical abstraction. It demands concrete pedagogical responses: teachers who function as murabbi and not merely instructors; curricula that are tawhid-integrated and not dualistically fragmented; learning environments that nurture the qalb alongside the aql; and assessment frameworks that evaluate character and spirituality alongside academic competency. Islamic educational psychology, at its best, is the science of forming the souls of the next generation in fidelity to the divine trust.
